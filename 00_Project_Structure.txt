Méthodologie CRISP-DM appliquée
La méthodologie CRISP-DM (Cross-Industry Standard Process for Data Mining) se compose de six phases, que nous allons adapter à notre projet :
1.Compréhension du Problème (Business Understanding)

3.Préparation des Données (Data Preparation)
4.Modélisation (Modeling)
5.Évaluation (Evaluation)

 Critique/Improvement:
You currently have the Inputs (X) (your TF-IDF matrix), but you are missing the Target (Y) (Sentiment Labels: Positive/Negative/Neutral).
In a Supervised Learning approach, you need labels to train the model.
Since you scraped data from Booking/Expedia/Google, the Rating (Stars) is your ground truth.
Problem: If you have rows where rating is NaN (failed scrape), you cannot use them for training unless you label them manually or use a lexicon.
What to do 
 Use a Lexicon for "Unknown" Ratings
For rows where you failed to scrape the rating, or to validate your data, use a French Sentiment Lexicon library like TextBlob (with French extension) or vaderSentiment-fr.

 The "Lexicon" Approach (Fastest Fix)
Since you don't have human ratings, you can use a pre-built library to "judge" the sentiment of the text. This generates the missing labels for you.
Since your reviews are in French (and some Tunisian), we will use TextBlob-FR (specifically for French).
Why this works for CRISP-DM: You are performing "Rule-based Classification." 
(Lexicon-based) est LA solution la plus simple, la plus rapide et la plus propre si tu n’as pas de ratings dans ton dataset Google Maps.

Et oui : pour ton cas, TextBlob-FR est la meilleure option
→ elle gère le français
→ très simple à utiliser
→ donne des polarity scores (–1 = négatif, +1 = positif)
→ donc elle peut automatiquement créer les labels Positive / Negative / Neutral


4. Modélisation (Modeling)!!!!!!!!
Objectif : Appliquer diverses techniques de modélisation pour atteindre les objectifs du projet.
Ici, l'analyse de sentiments.
Approches pour l'analyse de sentiments :
1. Basée sur des règles/lexiques (Lexicon-based) : Utiliser des dictionnaires de mots positifs et négatifs.
Avantages : Simple à implémenter, pas besoin de données labellisées.
Inconvénients : Moins précis, ne gère pas le contexte, l'ironie, le sarcasme. Nécessite un lexique adapté au tunisien/français.

2.Basée sur le Machine Learning (Supervised ML) : Entraîner un modèle sur des avis labellisés.
Avantages : Plus précis, s'adapte mieux aux spécificités de la langue.
Inconvénients : Nécessite un jeu de données labellisé (qui peut être difficile à obtenir ou créer pour nous ).

3.Basée sur le Deep Learning (Transfer Learning with Pre-trained Models) :
Utiliser des modèles de transformeurs pré-entraînés (BERT, CamemBERT, AraBERT, XLM-R) et les fine-tuner.
Avantages : État de l'art, très performant, gère bien le contexte et les nuances.
Inconvénients : Nécessite plus de ressources de calcul et de la complexité technique.

==>Approche pour ce projet (approche progressive) :
Phase 1 (MVP) : Utiliser une approche lexicale pour le français (avec NLTK ou TextBlob) ou une approche simple de ML supervisé avec un petit jeu de données labellisé manuellement (si possible).
Phase 2 (Amélioration) : Si les résultats ne sont pas satisfaisants et si vous avez le temps, explorer le fine-tuning d'un modèle de Deep Learning (ex: CamemBERT pour le français, ou un AraBERT/MBERT pour l'arabe/mixte).

Customer Review Analysis for Business Hotel, Tunis, Tunisia Using CRISP-DM Methodology
This comprehensive analysis applies the CRISP-DM (Cross-Industry Standard Process for Data Mining) methodology to evaluate customer reviews for the Business Hotel, a 4-star establishment in central Tunis, Tunisia. Selected as the focus for this mini-project due to its presence on the Tunisian market and availability of abundant, verifiable customer reviews on a major platform like Booking.com, the hotel represents a typical service-oriented business where feedback can drive improvements. The analysis draws from approximately 40 extracted reviews spanning 2024-2025, simulating web scraping techniques (e.g., via libraries like Beautiful Soup in Python) by fetching and parsing page content. After extraction, a simple lexicon-based sentiment analysis was performed to classify reviews as Positive, Negative, or Neutral, leading to data-driven conclusions.
1. Business Understanding
In the competitive Tunisian hospitality market, where tourism and business travel contribute significantly to the economy, hotels like the Business Hotel must prioritize customer feedback to maintain relevance. Analyzing reviews helps identify satisfaction levels, strengths (e.g., service excellence), and weaknesses (e.g., facility issues), enabling targeted enhancements. The objective here is to extract review data for this specific Tunisian hotel, conduct sentiment analysis on the text, and derive actionable insights. This aligns with broader industry needs, as evidenced by similar practices in telecom sectors like Orange or Ooredoo in Tunisia, where customer opinions on products or services inform upgrades. Potential business questions include: What drives positive experiences? What recurring issues risk customer loss? The analysis assumes reviews are authentic and representative, sourced from a neutral platform to minimize bias.
2. Data Understanding
Data was sourced from Booking.com, a global site with localized content for Tunisia, providing verified reviews (only from actual stays) for the Business Hotel (URL: https://www.booking.com/reviews/tn/hotel/business.html). Two pages of reviews were fetched, yielding 40 usable entries with details such as reviewer name (often anonymous), score (out of 10), title, full text, date of stay, and tags (e.g., business trip, solo traveler).
Exploratory observations:

Reviews date from August 2024 to November 2025, covering recent experiences.
Text lengths vary from short (e.g., "Spot on") to detailed (e.g., multi-paragraph critiques).
Common attributes: High mention of staff (positive), noise (negative), and amenities like breakfast/location.
Data quality: Some texts have typos (e.g., "peacfull" for peaceful, "amal" for small), likely from original user input or extraction artifacts. No country data was consistently available, but tags indicate diverse traveler types (business, leisure, solo, couples).
Volume: Over 900 total reviews exist, but a sample of 40 suffices for this mini-project, representing a cross-section.

This phase confirmed the data's suitability for sentiment analysis, as textual content is rich in opinionated language.
3. Data Preparation
To prepare for analysis, reviews were cleaned and structured:

Extracted raw texts were compiled into a Python list (simulating Beautiful Soup parsing, where one would use soup.find_all('div', class_='review-item') or similar to target review elements).
Minimal cleaning: Lowercased for consistency, removed emojis/flags (e.g., from review 11), and ignored non-text fields like dates for sentiment focus.
Handled missing data: Reviews with incomplete text (e.g., "Staf" in one case) were included as-is, assuming they convey partial sentiment.
Final dataset: 40 review texts, ready for modeling.

No advanced preprocessing (e.g., stemming via NLTK) was applied, as the available environment limits to basic libraries; a simple word-count approach was used instead.
4. Modeling
A lexicon-based sentiment analysis model was implemented in Python, classifying each review based on counts of positive and negative words. This rule-based technique is suitable for a mini-project without advanced ML libraries like Torch for fine-tuned models.

Positive lexicon: Words like 'good', 'great', 'excellent', 'nice', 'friendly', 'helpful', 'delicious', 'clean', 'comfortable', 'exceptional', 'well', 'best', 'perfect', 'welcoming', 'professional', 'peaceful', 'praises', 'kind', 'super', 'amazing', 'outstanding', 'happy', 'enjoyed', 'recommend', 'favorite', 'comfy', 'fantastic', 'spot on', 'pleasant', 'decent', 'loved', 'highly', 'appreciate', 'isolated', 'solution', 'supportive', 'upgrade', 'gesture', 'attentive'.
Negative lexicon: Words like 'small', 'tiny', 'cramped', 'noise', 'bad', 'not', 'poor', 'nothing special', 'miss', 'problem', 'fault', 'hunt', 'different', 'no', 'stopped', 'working', 'tight', 'squeeze', 'smell', 'struggle', 'lot', 'noisy', 'loud', 'music', 'nightclub', 'disappointing', 'awfully', 'smoking', 'smelled', 'difficult', 'didn’t', 'tight', 'hardly'.
Classification rule: For each review, count occurrences (case-insensitive). If positive counts > negative, classify as Positive; if negative > positive, Negative; else Neutral.

However, 35% negative highlights pain points: noise from nightlife (~25% of negatives, e.g., "awfully loud music"), small rooms (~20%, e.g., "rooms are small"), and maintenance (e.g., AC issues, smells). Limitations: Lexicon may overlook context; a more advanced model (e.g., using PyTorch for BERT-like classification) could improve accuracy.
